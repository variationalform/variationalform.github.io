Exercise 3¶

When studying regression we defined the notion of cost as, for example, total squared error, TSE, with regularization:

$$ \mathcal{E}(\boldsymbol{\theta}) = \Vert\boldsymbol{y}-\boldsymbol{X}\boldsymbol{\theta}\Vert_2^2 +\alpha\Vert\boldsymbol{\theta}\Vert_p^p $$

for $p=2$ (ridge) or $p=1$ (LASSO). This regularization term is often referred to as a penalty term in the sense that it penalises large coeffcients.

Given that we are trying to minimise cost it is clear that with $\alpha>0$ we will also be trying to minimize $\alpha\Vert\boldsymbol{\theta}\Vert_p^p$ which, in turn, tends to introduce small coefficients.

More than that though, the choice $p=1$ may introduce sparsity into the coefficent vector. This is a form of feature selection.

We can see why the $\ell_1$ norm seems to promote sparsity by borrowing from the discussion here: https://stats.stackexchange.com/questions/45643/why-l1-norm-for-sparse-models

  1. Define the vector $\boldsymbol{v}=(1,\epsilon)^T$ for some small $\epsilon > 0$ and show that $\Vert\boldsymbol{v}\Vert_1 = 1+\epsilon$ and $\Vert\boldsymbol{v}\Vert_2^2=1+\epsilon^2$.

  2. Assume that regularization reduces the first coefficent by $\delta$ (with $0<\delta <\epsilon <1$) so that $\boldsymbol{v}$ becomes $\boldsymbol{v}=(1-\delta,\epsilon)^T$. Show that now,

$$ \Vert\boldsymbol{v}\Vert_1 = 1-\delta+\epsilon \qquad\text{and}\qquad \Vert\boldsymbol{v}\Vert_2^2=1-2\delta+\delta^2+\epsilon^2. $$
  1. Now, assume instead that regularization reduces the second coefficent by $\delta$ (with $0<\delta <\epsilon < 1$) so that $\boldsymbol{v}$ becomes $\boldsymbol{v}=(1,\epsilon-\delta)^T$. Show that now,
$$ \Vert\boldsymbol{v}\Vert_1 = 1+\epsilon-\delta \qquad\text{and}\qquad \Vert\boldsymbol{v}\Vert_2^2=1+\epsilon^2-2\epsilon\delta+\delta^2. $$

  1. Is the reduction in norm the same in both cases for the $\ell_1$ norm?

  2. Is the reduction in norm the same in both cases for the $\ell_2$ norm? If not, for which is the reduction largest?

  3. Explain what the author on the website (as above) means when s/he says

it's not so much that $\ell_1$ penalties encourage sparsity, but that $\ell_2$ penalties in some sense discourage sparsity...

Answer 1¶

Here $\Omega = \{1,2,3,4,5,6\}$ and $\mathcal{E}$ has $2^{\vert\Omega\vert}=2^6$ elements.

Appealing to the symmetry of the dice and the impartiality of the laws of physics, we have that

  • $\mathrm{Prob}(\{6\}) = 1/6$, because all six numbers are equally likely and exactly one must occur.

  • $\mathrm{Prob}(\{2,4,6\}) = 3/6 = 1/2$ or

    • $\mathrm{Prob}(\{2\})+\mathrm{Prob}(\{4\})+\mathrm{Prob}(\{6\}) = 1/6 + 1/6 + 1/6 = 1/2$.

  • $\mathrm{Prob}(\{3,5\}) = 2/6 = 1/3$.

    • $\mathrm{Prob}(\{3,5\}) = \mathrm{Prob}(\{1,3,5\})\times\mathrm{Prob}(\{3,4,5,6\}) = 3/6 \times 4/6 = 1/3$.

The second illustrates that

  • $\mathrm{P}(A\mathrm{\ and\ } B) = \mathrm{P}(A)+\mathrm{P}(B)$ when $A$ and $B$ are mutually exclusive.

Answer 2¶

The way to discredit the prosecution's probability is to focus on the DNA testing lab's failure rate. Let $M$ be the event of a reported match between two samples. Let $T$ be the event of a true match, and let $F$ be the event of a falsely reported match. Then, $P(T)=10^{-6}$, $P(M)=P(T\cup F)=P(T)+P(F)=10^{-6}+10^{-5}$ and so,

$$ \mathrm{P}(T\mid M)=\frac{P(T\cap M)}{P(M)}=\frac{P(T)}{P(M)} =\frac{10^{-6}}{10^{-6}+10^{-5}}=\frac{1}{11}. $$

Another way of saying this is that, if we test every one of the $2,000,000$ women who may be guilty, we can expect $2$ (one in a million) to show a true match and the lab to falsely report another $20$ (one in $100,000$) as also matching. Hence, of the $22$ who are reported to match, only $2$ actually match and $2/22=1/11 < 10\%$ as above.

This means, we can imagine telling the jury, that there is a less than 10% probability that the defendant's DNA matches the DNA found at the crime scene. Moreover, in the absence of other evidence, there are 22 other defendant candidates and, by choosing one at random, the probability that we have the guilty one is only $1/22$, or about 4.5%.

Answer 3¶

  1. $\boldsymbol{v}=(1,\epsilon)^T$ and so $\Vert\boldsymbol{v}\Vert_1 = \sum_i\vert v_i\vert = 1+\epsilon$ and $\Vert\boldsymbol{v}\Vert_2^2 = \sum_i\vert v_i\vert^2 = 1+\epsilon^2$.

  2. $\boldsymbol{v}=(1-\delta,\epsilon)^T$ and so $\Vert\boldsymbol{v}\Vert_1 = \sum_i\vert v_i\vert = 1-\delta+\epsilon$ and $\Vert\boldsymbol{v}\Vert_2^2 = \sum_i\vert v_i\vert^2 = (1-\delta)^2+\epsilon^2 = 1-2\delta+\delta^2+\epsilon^2$.

  3. $\boldsymbol{v}=(1,\epsilon-\delta)^T$ and so $\Vert\boldsymbol{v}\Vert_1 = \sum_i\vert v_i\vert = 1+\epsilon-\delta$ and $\Vert\boldsymbol{v}\Vert_2^2 = \sum_i\vert v_i\vert^2 = 1+(\epsilon-\delta)^2 = 1+\epsilon^2-2\epsilon\delta+\delta^2$.

  4. Yes, the norm is reduced by $\delta$ in both cases.

  5. The $\ell_2$ norm is reduced by $(2-\delta)\delta$ when the larger component is reduced, but only by $(2\epsilon-\delta)\delta$ when the smaller component is reduced.

  6. Sparsity results from zeros being introduced into the coeffcient vector. A small value need only be reduced by a correspondingly small amount to become zero. The argument above suggests that small values only get a correspondingly small reduction in the $\ell_2$ norm, but get equal reduction as larger values in the $\ell_1$ norm. This leads us to believe that there is an increased possibility of zeroes being introduced in the LASSO case.

Answer 4¶

The midpoint is $\hat{\boldsymbol{x}} = (\hat{x}_1,\hat{x}_2) = \frac{1}{2}\Big(\boldsymbol{q}+\boldsymbol{r}\Big)$ and the straight line must go through that point.

The straight line must be orthogonal to $\boldsymbol{q}-\boldsymbol{r}$ and so its gradient must be $m=(q_1-r_1)/(r_2-q_2)$.

This line has the general form of equation,

$$ x_2 - \hat{x}_2 = \left(\frac{q_1-r_1}{r_2-q_2}\right)(x_1-\hat{x}_1). $$

In the specific case given we have $\hat{\boldsymbol{x}} = (\hat{x}_1,\hat{x}_2) = \frac{1}{2}\Big(5+2,1+3\Big)= \frac{1}{2}(7,4)$, and with $\boldsymbol{q} = (5,1)$ and $\boldsymbol{r} = (2,3)$ we have,

$$ x_2 - \frac{4}{2} = \left(\frac{5-2}{3-1}\right)\left(x_1-\frac{7}{2}\right). $$

Or, simplified,

$$ x_2 = \frac{3}{2}x_1-\frac{3}{2}\frac{7}{2} + \frac{4}{2} = \frac{3}{2}x_1-\frac{21}{4} + \frac{8}{4} = \frac{3}{2}x_1-\frac{13}{4}. $$

Answer 6¶

We use the formula we derived. With $\boldsymbol{q}=(2,1)^T$,

$$ d = \frac{\phi-\boldsymbol{w}^T\boldsymbol{q}}{\Vert\boldsymbol{w}\Vert_2} = \frac{1-(-1,1)(2,1)^T}{\sqrt{2}} = \frac{2}{\sqrt{2}} $$

and so $d=\surd 2$.

Answer 11¶

We have in general...

$$ \boldsymbol{x} = (x_0, x_1, \ldots, x_N)^T, \boldsymbol{y} = (y_0, y_1, \ldots, y_M)^T, \boldsymbol{y} = \sigma(\boldsymbol{W}^T\boldsymbol{x}+\boldsymbol{b}) $$

Here for a decision boundary of $-7x_1 + 3x_2 + 4=0$ we choose $\boldsymbol{W} = (-7, 3)^T$ and $b$ = 4. Then

$$ y = \mathcal{H}(\boldsymbol{W}^T\boldsymbol{x}+b). $$

Note that both $y$ and $b$ are scalars here. The equation of the decision in boundary can also be written as $x_2=(7x_1-4)/3$.
